# Professional Memoir: Darwin Hernandez
## A Career Built on Building Things

---

### Chapter 1: The Foundation — Caracas, Venezuela

Darwin Hernandez grew up in Caracas, Venezuela, the son of a blue-collar painter who colored streets, parks, and hospitals, and a mother who ironed and sewed clothing for priests at UCAB, one of Venezuela's most respected Catholic universities. Neither parent worked in business. Neither had a corporate career. But both modeled something more valuable: the dignity of work, and the understanding that effort is the one variable you control.

Darwin's original dream was engineering. He wasn't accepted into that program. Instead, he enrolled in accounting at Universidad Central de Venezuela — not a fallback, but a recalibration. He discovered that accounting gave him something engineering might not have: a precise language for how organizations actually work. A P&L tells a story. A balance sheet reveals decisions. Numbers don't lie, but they require someone who knows how to read them.

He graduated in 2007 with a B.S. in Accounting. He was already working.

---

### Chapter 2: Deloitte — Learning to See Backwards and Forwards

January 2006 to November 2009. Three years as an auditor at Deloitte, one of the most rigorous professional training grounds in the world. Darwin joined before finishing his degree and left as Senior Auditor.

Deloitte taught him two things that would define the next two decades:

First, analytical rigor. Darwin learned to pressure-test assumptions, build three-year financial projections, model equity movements, and find the story inside the numbers. Most people's estimates, he learned, are guesses dressed up as facts. Real numbers require discipline.

Second, direction. Auditing is backward-looking — it tells you what happened. Darwin realized he wanted to understand what *could* happen. What decisions create the future. That realization sent him toward marketing, toward strategy, toward the space where insight meets execution.

He also pursued a Master's in Marketing at UCAB and a Diploma in Innovation at Universidad Metropolitana during this period — seeds of systems thinking that would grow quietly for years before becoming his professional identity.

---

### Chapter 3: Telefonica — Scale, National and Personal

December 2009 to July 2015. Darwin joined Telefonica Venezuela as a marketer, eventually leading the prepaid consumer segment — a massive, complex motion involving nationwide direct marketing across more than 50,000 points of sale. Pharmacies. Convenience stores. Street vendors. A national distribution network for a product millions of Venezuelans used every day.

This was marketing at a scale most practitioners never touch. Darwin learned how to build campaigns for segments, not just customers. How to think in distribution channels. How to move a number — customer acquisition, retention, market share — across an entire country.

But Venezuela was changing. The economy was deteriorating. Scarcity. Insecurity. Poverty spreading visibly. Darwin and his wife had a three-year-old daughter. They made the kind of decision that only parents in crisis fully understand: they would leave. They applied to graduate programs in the United States as their entry strategy — not escape, but a calculated move toward a better future.

They were accepted. They packed up their lives and moved to Monroe, Louisiana.

---

### Chapter 4: Louisiana — The Hardest and Most Important Chapter

It was the three of them. Darwin, his wife, their daughter. Short on money. More bills than they had ever faced. A culture they didn't yet understand — more individualistic than what they knew, less communal, quieter in ways that felt isolating.

Christmas was especially hard. While their families gathered thousands of miles away in Venezuela, Darwin, his wife, and their daughter spent the holidays alone. No extended family. No familiar traditions. Just the three of them, and the understanding that this was their only opportunity. They could not fail.

Darwin enrolled at the University of Louisiana at Monroe MBA program, on campus, full-time in spirit if not always in presence. To cover tuition and fees, he secured a Business Advisor assistantship at the Louisiana Small Business Development Center (LSBDC). Local entrepreneurs came in with rough ideas — a food truck concept, a retail store, a service business. Darwin's job was to translate those ideas into viable business plans: TAM/SAM analysis, three-year financial projections, P&Ls, cash flow models, initial capital estimates. His boss would review and counsel the entrepreneur. Darwin built the financial architecture that made the counseling possible.

Then, in early 2016, Darwin conceived SOS Me.

The idea came from observation. People were busy. They needed things — groceries, takeout, prescriptions — but couldn't always leave their homes or offices. What if an app could connect people who needed errands run (Requesters) with people who had time and a car (Runners)? The Requester uploads a shopping list. The Runner accepts, buys, delivers. Payment processes automatically through the app. Both parties rate each other.

Darwin built a complete business model: 4 Ps, SWOT analysis, Business Model Canvas, financial projections, pricing model (10% commission each to Runner and platform). He presented it to his MBA professor, Dr. Babin. The professor told him to execute it.

Darwin couldn't. His schedule — MBA coursework, LSBDC assistantship, parenting, marriage, survival — left no room for a startup. He filed the idea away.

Two years later, Uber Eats and Instacart scaled nationally. The model Darwin had architected in his home, during what were the hardest years of his life, became a multi-billion dollar category.

He graduated with a 4.0 GPA in 2017.

The lesson he took from SOS Me was not regret. It was confirmation: he could see markets before they existed. He could build conceptual architectures from scratch. He just needed the right environment to execute.

---

### Chapter 5: CenturyLink/Lumen — The Long Build

Darwin entered CenturyLink (later rebranded Lumen Technologies) through an MBA internship in 2016. He converted it into a full-time Lead Marketing Analyst role. He was thirty-something, newly graduated, building a career in a country that wasn't his birthplace, at a company going through its own identity crisis.

He stayed for nearly a decade. Not out of inertia — out of faith, and because the scope kept expanding.

**2016–2019: Analyst to Chief of Staff**

Darwin started in analytics — dashboards, forecasting, persona-based campaigns for the midsize business segment. He learned the company's architecture, its products, its internal politics. He was good at making sense of complexity, and leadership noticed.

He was appointed Chief of Staff to the VP of Small Business Group — a role that had nothing to do with his job description and everything to do with his instinct for creating order from ambiguity. He improved planning cadence, reduced planning timelines by 25%, and built Salesforce and analytics dashboards that gave executives real-time GTM visibility.

He also led GTM for fiber broadband — a full launch motion that achieved over 100% YoY revenue growth through persona-driven messaging and integrated campaigns. His first real taste of end-to-end GTM ownership. He liked it.

**2022–2024: Cybersecurity PMM — The Rebuild**

In February 2022, Darwin was handed a neglected portfolio of five professional security services: Managed SIEM, Virtual SOC, Pen Testing, Vulnerability Assessment, and later DDoS Mitigation. No existing PMM support. No message maps. No assets. No positioning.

He rebuilt everything from scratch.

He developed message maps and tested them — not just internally, but with the analysts who mattered: Gartner, IDC, Frost & Sullivan. Darwin walked into those briefing rooms not as a vendor seeking commissioned research, but as a peer presenting original thinking. "Here's what we're seeing. Here's our positioning. What do you think?" He kept the thought leadership internal and original. The analyst relationships became a card he could play when needed.

He validated messaging with field sellers. He produced assets. He trained the sales organization. The results were real: 70%+ asset adoption across the sales org. ~10% ARR growth. 10% YoY increase in closed-won deals.

DDoS Mitigation became the strategic anchor of the portfolio — not because it was the most technically sophisticated product, but because Darwin recognized something the organization hadn't fully articulated: DDoS was the natural bridge between Lumen's core connectivity identity and its security ambitions. Black Lotus Labs' Rapid Threat Defense intelligence layer gave it differentiation no competitor could easily replicate. Darwin used it as the foundation for what would become the bundle GTM motion.

Then, in January 2024, something happened that had nothing to do with his job description.

**Christmas Break 2023: The Copilot Olympics**

Darwin was in his home office during the Christmas break. Lumen had joined Microsoft 365 Copilot's Early Access Program in spring 2023 — one of the first companies to do so. But adoption was uneven. People had the tool. They weren't using it.

Darwin had an idea: make adoption fun. Turn it into a competition.

He came back in January 2024, pitched it to his manager, got her blessing, and ran the first Copilot Olympics by the end of January — less than four weeks from concept to execution. Marketing teams competed to create branding and materials for fictional products (indestructible clothing for parents, an inflatable car, a dream recorder) using only Copilot, within 40 minutes.

The event worked. It grew. Four events within his team, then a company-wide event across Lumen's entire 250-person marketing organization. Teams competed, collaborated, built a company-wide prompt library. Every element of the event — judging criteria, guides, images — was produced using Copilot.

In July 2024, Microsoft's editorial newsroom published a feature on Lumen's AI transformation. Darwin was named and quoted as the person who conceived and organized Copilot Olympics. His CMO was in the same article. Darwin was the only Lumen marketer recognized by name.

He had invented a program, during the holidays, in his home office, that scaled to 250 people and earned external recognition from one of the world's most valuable companies.

This is who Darwin is.

**2024–Present: Bundles, LVDs, and the Synthesis**

Darwin's current role brought together everything that came before.

He led GTM for four enterprise bundle solutions — DIA + DDoS, DIA + SASE, IP VPN + SASE, DIA + Cloud Voice. Each bundle required a complete GTM kit: message map, datasheet, presentation, battlecard, conversation guide, email template, landing page. Four bundles. Thirty-plus assets. All built from scratch.

The bundles solved a real customer problem Darwin had diagnosed himself: the experience of buying multiple Lumen products was fragmented. Services delivered without coordination. Billing before deployment was complete. Value understated. Bundles fixed the customer journey — buy, get, pay — while articulating the joint value of combined services and making the case for consolidation with a single trusted provider.

To get there, Darwin had to bridge a structural misalignment between product managers (who owned individual products) and segment product teams (who sponsored the bundle initiative). They weren't aligned. Darwin created forums and GTM meetings specifically to connect the two factions. He became the alignment layer — not because he had authority over either group, but because he understood that PMM's real job is to create structure where none exists.

He also co-led the development of Lumen's enterprise AI framework — an interactive, multi-section thought leadership asset published on lumen.com. He received a vague brief. He built an interactive PowerPoint that mapped the sections, navigation, and user experience. He pitched it to creative, copywriters, digital marketing, growth, and product teams — fifteen people in total. He orchestrated the kickoff and alignment, then handed off to a Product Manager once the plan was set. The asset shipped.

Then came the Validated Designs.

Darwin became PMM owner for Lumen's Validated Designs (LVD) program — a strategic initiative positioning pre-tested, multi-vendor architectures that solve specific enterprise business problems. The value proposition is elegant: less complexity (someone already tested that this architecture works), faster adoption (no blueprinting from scratch), and cost savings (less rework).

His flagship LVD: Cyber Resilience with Commvault. Lumen provides secure, high-speed data transport (Wavelengths + IP VPN, 100+ Gbps) from air-gapped storage to production environments during a cyber event. Commvault provides the air-gapped storage structure. The architecture has been validated in Lumen's own production environment — Lumen doesn't just sell this solution, they run their business on it.

Darwin owns the messaging, the enablement assets, the thought leadership, and the partner coordination with Commvault. He is also involved in three additional LVDs in Q1 2026, and is building the foundational content and education infrastructure for the entire program — internal and external.

The through-line from SOS Me in 2016 to Lumen Validated Designs in 2026 is the same brain. Darwin thinks in platforms and ecosystems. He sees how multiple components — services, vendors, customer journeys, internal teams — can be orchestrated into something more valuable than the sum of their parts. He received that insight while designing a two-sided marketplace for grocery delivery in Louisiana. He is now applying it to enterprise infrastructure architectures at global scale.

### Chapter 6: Who Darwin Is — The Honest Assessment

Darwin is at his best when he receives ambiguity and has to build structure that others can execute against. The AI framework. The Copilot Olympics. The bundle GTM forums. The LVD program architecture. Every significant achievement in his career follows the same pattern: unclear brief → Darwin's structure → team execution → shipped output → measurable result.

He is analytically grounded in a field full of storytellers who can't do math. His Deloitte years, his LSBDC financial modeling, his dashboard work — these give his metrics credibility that most PMMs can't match. When Darwin says ~10% ARR growth, he knows what ARR means, where the number came from, and how to defend it.

He is an ecosystem thinker, not a product thinker. He doesn't ask "what does this product do?" He asks "what outcome does this combination of things solve for the customer, and how do we orchestrate the journey from awareness to purchase to value realization?"

He is a builder. Not a maintainer. Not a manager of existing motions. A builder — of messaging frameworks, of adoption programs, of alignment infrastructure, of validated architectures. He gets energy from creating things that didn't exist before.

And he is a person who has operated under real pressure — financial, cultural, familial — and converted that pressure into a 4.0 GPA, a decade of expanding scope at a major enterprise technology company, and a professional identity that is genuinely rare in the market: a solutions PMM who can architect ecosystem narratives, build the GTM infrastructure to support them, and enable an entire sales organization to execute.

He is ready for the next chapter.
